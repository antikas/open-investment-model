# OpenIM Service-Domain Model

The service-domain decomposition of an institutional investment manager — the buy-side: asset managers, sovereign wealth funds, LP allocators, institutional investors. Asset-class agnostic; covers public equities, fixed income, cash and money markets, private equity, private credit, real estate, infrastructure, natural resources and commodities, and hedge funds and active strategies — invested both through external managers and funds, and through direct and co-investments. (Secondaries and co-investment are transaction types over these classes, not asset classes — see [E-09 Asset Class](../entities/core/E-09-asset-class.md).)

This is the OpenIM equivalent of BIAN's Service Landscape.

> Vocabulary not familiar? The [glossary](../glossary.md) defines the investment-management terms used here (LP / GP / LPA, capital call, distribution, J-curve, vintage, the IRR / TVPI / DPI / RVPI family, waterfall and carry, GIPS / ILPA, and the rest).
>
> Visual: the [Business Domain map](../diagrams/02-business-domain-map.md) renders the 17 Business Domains grouped by office tag with Service-Domain counts. The full set of diagrams is in [`../diagrams/`](../diagrams/INDEX.md).

## How to read this model

OpenIM mirrors BIAN's *form* — a business is decomposed into discrete, non-overlapping units of capability — at a deliberately shallower depth. BIAN uses four levels (Business Area → Business Domain → Service Domain → Service Operation); OpenIM uses two structural levels (Business Domain → Service Domain), because the buy-side firm is a smaller enterprise than a universal bank. The third level — Service Operations — is decomposed within each Service Domain's own file (a `## Service Operations` section), not as a separate structural tier.

A **Service Domain** is a discrete, non-overlapping unit of business capability — a single thing the firm does, owning the full lifecycle of one asset or entity type. Service domains are *capabilities*, not organisational units: a real firm's departments each consume many service domains, and one service domain may be exercised by several departments.

**Office tag** is applied at Business-Domain level (Front / Middle / Back / Cross-cutting / Commercial), not per service domain — several service domains span offices, and forcing a per-domain office tag would break non-overlap.

**Applicability tag**, per service domain:
- `PUB` — applies to liquid / public-markets investing
- `PRIV` — applies to private-markets / fund / direct investing
- `BOTH` — applies to both

## What this model covers

OpenIM is a reference model for **investment management** — the buy-side whole. Public-equity, fixed-income, private-equity, private-credit, real-asset, infrastructure, hedge-fund and derivative capabilities are all parts of one model. An implementation activates the subset its mandate requires and leaves the rest dormant: a listed-equity manager activates BD-02 and the public capabilities of BD-05 / BD-06 / BD-08 / BD-09 / BD-12, and leaves BD-03 / BD-04 dormant; an LP allocator activates BD-03 and leaves BD-06's execution domains dormant.

This follows the BIAN precedent — BIAN's Service Landscape decomposes a generic universal bank, and an individual bank derives its own blueprint as a subset. The model is the union of capability; an implementation is a subset.

## Organised by capability, not by asset class

The model is two levels — Business Domain → Service Domain — with a per-Service-Domain applicability tag. It is organised by capability rather than by asset class, because a capability is the same capability whatever the asset class: "Performance Measurement" is one Service Domain whether the portfolio is listed equity or private equity. The `PUB` / `PRIV` / `BOTH` tag marks where a capability applies — where it is dormant for a given mandate — not a different capability. A tag never licenses asset-class-specific framing of a capability that should read generically; the asset-class balance review enforces that.

## Frequently omitted service domains

Decompositions of the buy-side routinely miss these — either because they are private-markets-specific (the LP operating model is far less standardised than the liquid-markets one) or because they are quiet operational functions. OpenIM names them explicitly:

1. **SD-01.10 Commitment Pacing & Deployment Planning** — the most-missed domain. Liquid-markets models have no equivalent; without it, illiquid allocations either never reach target or over-commit.
2. **SD-10.5 Side-Letter & Fund-Term Compliance** — MFN elections, excuse / exclusion rights and bespoke reporting clauses are negotiated per-LP and must be *enforced*. Usually collapsed into generic compliance and lost.
3. **SD-05.8 Portfolio Transition Management** — the structured movement of assets between managers, strategies or benchmarks. Has its own cost metric (implementation shortfall); usually buried inside trading.
4. **SD-12.13 Securities Lending Operations** — a revenue and operational-risk domain, with a hard interaction with proxy voting (recall before record date). Often treated as a custodian black box.
5. **SD-12.12 Proxy Voting & Stewardship Operations** — increasingly material under stewardship codes; often mis-filed under compliance or ESG.
6. **SD-07.5 Look-Through Exposure Analysis** — for a fund-investing institution, true risk only exists at look-through; without it, concentration and factor risk are systematically understated.
7. **SD-13.6 GP & Manager Report Ingestion** — the unstructured-data on-ramp for the entire private-markets book. Treated as a data-entry afterthought rather than a governed capability.
8. **SD-05.6 Liquidity-Aware Portfolio Management** — modelling the liquidity claim of undrawn commitments. The denominator-effect failures of 2022–23 were failures of this missing domain.
9. **SD-04.7 Co-Investment Management** — co-invest rights, allocation fairness, and the conflicts between fund and co-invest. A genuine capability with governance weight, usually unnamed.
10. **SD-03.3 Fund Operational Due Diligence (ODD)** — distinct from investment DD, independently staffed in mature institutions, and the control that catches fraud and service-provider risk. Routinely merged into investment diligence and thereby weakened.

## Design notes

- **Office tagging is at Business-Domain level.** Several service domains span offices (e.g. SD-08.3 Private-Asset Valuation has front-office input and middle-office governance). Forcing a per-domain office tag would break non-overlap.
- **Service domains are capabilities, not org units.** A real institution's asset-class departments (private equity, fixed income, infrastructure, etc.) each *consume* the BD-01 to BD-06 service domains; a portfolio-analytics department maps to BD-09, an investment-risk department to BD-07. The model deliberately does not mirror an org chart.
- **BD-01 is structured by institution archetype.** Investment strategy is the capability where buy-side institution type matters most — the unit of allocation is an asset-class weight for an asset owner, a model portfolio or product for a manager, a risk budget for a hedge fund, a liability-matched tranche for a pension or insurer. BD-01's thirteen Service Domains split a common spine (SD-01.1–01.5) from archetype-activated expressions (SD-01.6–01.13). The institution-archetype balance review guards every BD expansion against modelling one archetype's paradigm as the universal case — the institution-type analogue of the asset-class balance reviewer.
- **The OCIO / fiduciary-manager archetype activation across the model.** The OCIO is a *mandate-origin* archetype — it operates the BD subset of whichever asset-owner client it serves, under the `inbound-delegated` mandate-origin pattern codified in **SD-01.2 Investment Mandate & Policy Definition**. **Unless a BD's archetype-activation table or design note states otherwise, the OCIO's activation across any BD is "the third-party asset manager's activation, applied to the delegated portfolios"** — the OCIO runs BD-05 portfolio construction, BD-06 execution, BD-07 risk, BD-10 compliance, BD-11 treasury, BD-12 operations, BD-14 enterprise risk and control, and BD-17 corporate services on its delegated books as a regulated firm in its own right; the OCIO is **subject to its own regulator on its own books, to the client's IPS on the client's books**. BD-specific deviations from this default are recorded in the relevant BD README's design notes. The substantive deviations are: **BD-01** (the strategy domain — the OCIO authors strategy *as delegated discretion*, codified in SD-01.2's `inbound-delegated` operation), **BD-03** (manager selection *is* the saleable product — the inversion BD-03 records), **BD-09** (performance — GIPS-composite presentation as for an asset manager), **BD-12** (operations interface with the administrator), and **BD-15** (distribution — the OCIO *sells* the capability through institutional sales and consultant-mediated RFPs; the OCIO is active in BD-15 in a way the asset-owner is not). The five BDs the default subset above does not name activate as follows: **BD-02** the OCIO does not insource — the OCIO selects managers (BD-03), who in turn run BD-02 themselves; BD-02 is dormant on the OCIO's own books by default (and the OCIO's BD-02 activation, if it operates a directly-managed sleeve at all, is then the third-party asset manager's subset on that sleeve only). **BD-04** is dormant by default — the OCIO does not lead direct deals; an OCIO operating a client-funded co-investment programme would activate BD-04 as the niche case, otherwise dormant. **BD-08** the OCIO consumes valuations from administrators and custodians and reviews private-marks via SD-08.3, producing the carrying valuation on the delegated books — the third-party asset manager's subset applied to delegated portfolios. **BD-13** the OCIO operates the BD-13 data-and-reporting capability on the delegated books as a third-party asset manager would, with the SD-13.10 reporting flow feeding the OCIO → client communication chain (the OCIO's outbound BD-15 client reporting consumes BD-13's outputs). **BD-16** the OCIO has its own firm-level governance (board, committees) on its own books for its own accountability; the substantive client-facing accountability stream the OCIO produces per the OCIO mandate flows through SD-16.2 owner-and-investor reporting, directed to the client and its governing body, distinct from the OCIO's own board reporting.
- **The panel-substitution discipline.** The default archetype panel across BD READMEs is the seven-archetype spine of the institution-archetype balance review: **TPAM / DBP / SWF-E / INS / WM / OCIO / HF**. A BD may deviate from the default along three legitimate axes, each requiring an explicit one-line rationale in the BD README's design notes (the rationale requirement applies to all three axes): (a) **sub-archetype expansion** — adding finer-grained sub-archetypes where the differentiation runs at that grain. The applicable BD list (**BD-03** and **BD-04**) is exhaustive; the row enumerations below are illustrative of the discriminating axes — the full row sets (including dormancy markers and spine relabellings) live in each BD's archetype-activation table. **BD-03**'s discriminating axes: a separate "Fund-of-funds / multi-manager" row, a separate "Endowment & foundation" row, and a "Sovereign wealth fund / large asset owner" row in place of what would otherwise be the asset-owner collapse default. **BD-04**'s discriminating axes: "Large pension — Canadian model", "Sovereign wealth fund (savings / strategic)", "Family office (sophisticated single-family)", "Specialist real-assets manager". (b) **Asset-owner collapse** — collapsing DBP and SWF-E (the non-insurer asset-owner archetypes) into one "Asset owner (pension / SWF / endowment)" row where the BD's discriminating axis does not run between them; the **insurer (INS) is not part of the collapse** and keeps its own row in every BD applying this rule. The eleven BDs that currently apply the collapse — **BD-02, BD-05 (as a column in the SD × archetype matrix), BD-06, BD-07, BD-08, BD-10, BD-11, BD-14, BD-15 (collapsed-into-Dormant), BD-16, BD-17** — record the rationale in their design notes. (c) **Manager-substyle substitution** — substituting a manager-substyle cut for the archetype panel where the substyle is the discriminating axis (BD-02's fundamental / quant / credit / long-short cut), with a one-line design-notes rationale for the substyle choice itself and the existing one-line note acknowledging the absent archetypes' activation via the landscape rule above.
- **BD-02 boundaries.** BD-02 Securities Research & Selection *produces* researched, recommended securities with a conviction view; BD-05 Portfolio Management *constructs* the portfolio from them — the same producer / consumer split as BD-03 and BD-04 feeding BD-05. BD-02's research is bottom-up and security-level; SD-01.3 Capital Market Assumptions & House View is top-down macro. SD-02.6 owns the live research thesis on a security; SD-05.2 monitors the held position against the mandate.
- **Secondaries** is not a separate Business Domain: GP-led and LP-led secondary transactions are handled by SD-03.5 (commitment), SD-04.x (the direct purchase), SD-12.8 (the funds flow) and SD-08.3 (valuation). A dedicated Secondary Transaction Processing service domain is an open extension if practitioners find the cross-cutting handling insufficient.
- **Manager structure is portfolio construction, not manager selection.** SD-05.10 Manager Structure constructs the *portfolio of managers* — count, sizing, overlap, capacity, fund-roster diversification. It is distinct from BD-03, which sources, researches and selects the *individual* manager and feeds candidates to SD-05.10, and from SD-01.10 Commitment Pacing, which sets the timing and amount of commitment deployment SD-05.10's diversification design is then filled by.
- **Completeness cross-check.** The combined functional module lists of the major buy-side platforms (BlackRock Aladdin, SimCorp One, State Street Alpha / Charles River, SS&C / eFront, Bloomberg AIM, Clearwater) map cleanly onto BD-05 through BD-13, with no functional module unmapped. OpenIM also covers capability the liquid-markets vendor stacks do not — BD-03, BD-04, SD-01.10, SD-10.5 — the fund-investing and allocator domains those stacks were never built for.
- **Deliberately out of scope — generic enterprise and non-investment functions.** Several capabilities a buy-side firm exercises are *not* modelled as Service Domains, because they are generic enterprise functions with no investment-management-distinctive shape, or belong to a different industry function. Named here so the exclusion is explicit, not silent: **knowledge management** (a BIAN domain — for OpenIM a generic enterprise capability, not buy-side-distinctive); **generic corporate procurement** (SD-17.8 already covers the buy-side-distinctive slice — oversight of investment service providers such as custodians, administrators and data vendors; generic sourcing is out of scope); **change / transformation management** (a generic enterprise delivery capability); **corporate real estate & facilities** (the firm's own offices — distinct from real estate as an *asset class*, which is in scope via BD-04 and E-09); **pension-scheme member / benefit administration** (contributions, benefit payments and member servicing — a pension *scheme-administration* function; OpenIM models the investing of the assets, not the administration of the scheme's members); **corporate communications and press / media relations and internal employee communications** (a generic enterprise capability with no buy-side-distinctive shape — the firm's marketing-and-brand activity that *is* buy-side-distinctive is SD-15.5 Marketing, Brand & Content; the corporate-investor relations of a listed asset manager are governance / accountability, BD-16). Technology / IT management is **not** in this exclusion list — it *is* a buy-side capability, on the reasoning that a modern regulated investment manager is increasingly a technology business; the IT capability is modelled as the BD-17 plan/build/run trio (SD-17.7 / SD-17.9 / SD-17.10).
